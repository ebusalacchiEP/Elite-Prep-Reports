07Recommendations
1
Build a Dedicated Mental Skills System

You have 25 LT sessions with zero entries in categories #5 (Breathing/Meditation), #6 (Visualization), or #7 (Course Management & Strategy). Your mental mastery is rated 2/10 and unchanged across two check-ins.

Why we recommend this: Your data tells a clear story. Research consistently shows that pre-round routines, box breathing, and structured course management decision-making account for the majority of scoring improvement at your level.

LT #5 — Box Breathing: Inhale for 4 counts, hold for 4, exhale for 4, hold for 4 — that is one cycle, taking 16 seconds. Practice 5–10 cycles per session until it is automatic, then use it between shots on course to reset your nervous system before stepping into your routine.

LT #7 — Course Management Study: Pre-round target mapping, dispersion review, and expectation calibration practiced off the course so the correct decision is already made before you step onto the first tee.

2
Complete a Full Shot Pattern and Distance Mapping Session

Hit every club approximately 10 times on a launch monitor. Record not just average carry but the full dispersion — lateral spread left and right, and carry variance short and long. This builds a personal shot-pattern profile for every club in your bag.

How to use your patterns to pick targets on course: Compare the lateral distance between trouble areas off the tee, and do the same for green widths and depths. Then, overlay your personal shot patterns onto those areas. The ellipse should be aimed so that it, first and foremost, reduces the chance of a penalty shot or hazard. Then, progressively get more aggressive with targets the shorter the yardage is.

3
Replace Block Practice with Competitive Pressure and Random Reps

Your approach practice is currently 82% block. This produces technique acquisition but does not build the transfer skills needed in competition. The gap between your practice rounds and tournament rounds may be a result of a practice environment that does not replicate the demands of actual play.

Try to shift to at least 50% random practice — different club, target, and shot shape every rep with a full pre-shot routine. Add competitive pressure: scoring games and target challenges with consequences. Aim for pressure simulation in a minimum of 50% of approach and driving sessions (currently 24% and 11%).

4
Master Contact Quality and Swing-Length Control With Short Clubs

Your 13 Four<100 situations and 6 double-chip instances may be due to poor contact and distance control issues. Poor low-point control produces fat and thin strikes that miss the green. Inconsistent swing lengths inside 100 yards produce unpredictable carry distances that make it impossible to commit to a landing zone.

Focus on three specific areas: (1) Low-point control — consistent ball-first contact across varied lies; (2) Swing-length calibration — knowing exactly what a 25, 40, 60, 80, and 100-yard swing produces; (3) Routine consistency — the identical pre-shot process in practice and on the course. It may be worth having your instructor look at your wedge and pitching motion. The goal should be to ensure great contact every time.

5
Obsess Over Putting Distance Control

You averaged 1.8 three-putts per round — approximately 1.2 above his target of a 1 handicap.

Apply the 10% Rule in every session: your target on any putt outside 20 feet is a circle equal to 10% of the putt's distance — within 3 feet on a 30-footer, within 4 feet on a 40-footer.

Expectation management: It also helps to calibrate your expectations on shorter putts. Even a scratch golfer makes only around 50% of putts from 6 feet — this is a statistical reality, not a failure. Understanding that missing a makeable putt is normal, not a mental collapse, removes the self-criticism that often triggers a spiral of three-putts. Your goal inside 6 feet is not to make every putt — it is to give every putt the same committed, process-focused routine.
